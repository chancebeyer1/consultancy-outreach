# Landlord validation script — LA Rent Desk

**Who runs this:** Tanner
**Who it's for:** owners of 1–20 rental units in LA County (sphere, past clients, open-house contacts, referrals)
**Goal of every call:** find out how they handle rent increases *today*, whether it costs them money or sleep, and whether they'll put a card down for a $49/mo fix. You are validating, not selling. A clear "no, because ___" logged in the call log is a win.

**The one rule:** ask about what they've *done*, not what they *would do*. "Would you use this?" produces polite lies. "When did you last raise rent and how did you pick the number?" produces truth.

---

## 1. Who to call, in order

**Tier 1 — highest pain first:**
Anyone in your sphere who owns **2+ units inside City of LA limits in a pre-1978 building** (RSO). Remember most of the Valley — Van Nuys, North Hollywood, Reseda, Canoga Park — IS City of LA. Owners are routinely wrong about which rules apply to them; that confusion is the product.

**Tier 2:**
Owners of 1980s–2000s buildings anywhere in the county (AB 1482 — the cap resets every August 1), and anyone with property in **unincorporated** pockets: Altadena, East LA, View Park/Windsor Hills, Hacienda Heights, Marina peninsula adjacent. County has its own, stricter ordinance and almost nobody knows it.

**Tier 3:**
Single-house/condo landlords. Usually exempt — but only if they've put the right exemption language in the lease, which most haven't. Lower urgency; still worth 10 minutes.

## 2. First touch (text/DM — the job is to start a conversation, not to pitch)

Keep it native to who you already are to them. One question, no link, no pitch.

> "Hey [name] — you still have the [fourplex on Mariposa / duplex in NoHo]? Quick question about how you're handling the rent-increase reset this summer."

or, for someone you talk to less often:

> "[Name] — random one. Do you do your own rent-increase math on the [property], or does someone handle that for you? Asking because I keep seeing owners get it wrong and I'm trying to figure out if it's as common as it looks."

Do **not** open with the product, a link, or "can I get 15 minutes." The question earns the call.

## 3. The call

### Opener (10 seconds, honest)

> "Thanks — here's the context. Chance and I are building a tool that keeps LA rental owners on the right side of the rent-increase rules — the RSO / AB 1482 / county mess. Before we sink months into it, I'm pressure-testing it with people who actually own units. Can I ask you a few questions about how you do it today? Either way I'll give you this year's numbers for your building — they changed again this summer."

Notes: no "this isn't a pitch" disclaimer (it reads as a pitch). You ARE building something and you ARE asking for their experience — say exactly that.

### Discovery (the core — 80% listening, take verbatim notes)

Ask in this order. Follow the story wherever it goes; these are checkpoints, not a script to read aloud.

1. **"When did you last raise rent on [property]? How'd you land on the number?"**
   *(Listen for: guessed, copied a neighbor, asked their agent, called AAGLA, paid a lawyer, or — common — haven't raised since before COVID.)*

2. **"Walk me through how you checked what was allowed. How long did that take?"**
   *(Hours on Google and LAHD's site = pain. "My PM does it" = pivot to Q7.)*

3. **"Have you ever skipped or delayed an increase because you weren't sure what was legal?"**
   Then do the math with them, out loud:
   *(4 units × $1,900 rent × 4% skipped = **$3,648 this year** — and under RSO there's no catch-up. Skipped increases are gone permanently. Most owners don't know that.)*

4. **"Ever had a tenant, a tenant's lawyer, or LAHD push back on an increase or a notice?"**
   *(Get the whole story. What did it cost — refunds, rollback, attorney fees, the months of stress? Log quotes verbatim; these become the website's proof section later.)*

5. **"Who keeps track of your registration renewal and SCEP dates? Ever been hit with a late fee or a surprise inspection?"**
   *(A blank pause here is a signal — most self-managing owners have no system.)*

6. **"What do you actually spend on this today?"**
   *(AAGLA dues, attorney per-notice, PM fees, their own hours. This sets the price anchor before you ever say $49.)*

7. **If they have a property manager:** "Has your PM ever shown you the math on an allowed increase — like, which ordinance and what percentage? Do you know which of your units are RSO vs. state-cap?"
   *(If they trust the PM blindly: don't fight it. Ask which PM firm — that's a lead for the PM track — and move to the referral ask.)*

### Score it live (mental, log after)

- **HOT (3):** self-manages RSO units + has a horror story or skipped increases + no deadline system
- **WARM (2):** AB 1482 / county units, does it manually, annoyed at annual resets
- **LUKEWARM (1):** interested but delegates everything to a PM they trust
- **COLD (0):** exempt property, or genuinely zero involvement

### The demo (60 seconds, only if score ≥ 1)

> "Give me the building address."

Run it through the calculator (site on your phone) while they watch/listen:

> "Okay — that's City of LA, built '72, so you're RSO. Max increase right now is [X]%, [+1% if you pay their gas, +1% electric]. One increase per 12 months, 30-day written notice, and your registration renewal is due end of February. That took twenty seconds. The full desk also watches every rule change — city council changed the formula [reference from answer bank] — drafts the actual notice from attorney-reviewed templates, and keeps a per-unit deadline calendar so February never surprises you."

### The ask (ladder — always make ask A before softening)

**A — the card (the only signal that counts):**
> "We're taking 25 founding members at $49 a month — that's locked for life; plan price will be $79. Card goes on file today through Stripe, you're charged nothing until your desk actually goes live this fall, cancel anytime before or after. Founding members also get a direct line to us and shape what gets built first. Want in?"

**B — if they hesitate (this is where the real data is):**
> "No pressure either way — but tell me straight: what would have to be true for this to be a no-brainer at $49?"
Log the answer word for word. That sentence is the product roadmap.

**C — soft landing (always):**
> "Fair. Two small things then — can I text you the free calculator link so you have this year's numbers? And who else do you know with units in the city who fights with this every summer?"

Every call ends with the referral question, including the yeses.

## 4. Questions they'll fire back

Current percentages, deadlines, penalties, "isn't this legal advice," "my PM handles it," "I'll just Google it" — all in **[answer-bank.md](answer-bank.md)**. Read it twice before the first call; keep it open during calls.

## 5. Guardrails (protect yourself and the test)

- **Positioning, verbatim if needed:** "It's document preparation and deadline tracking built on attorney-reviewed templates — think TurboTax for rent increases, not a law firm. For a live dispute you want an attorney, and the tool will tell you that too."
- **Never** advise on an active dispute, an eviction, or a specific pending notice. Refer to their attorney or the AAGLA hotline. You are collecting facts about their process, not practicing law.
- Every number you quote comes from the answer bank with its as-of date. If you don't know: "I'll check and text you" beats improvising — wrong numbers are exactly the disease we're curing.
- Don't trash PMs or attorneys. PMs are our second customer; attorneys are our template reviewers.
- If they want to talk about selling the building instead — congratulations, that's your day job. Take the win and log the call as COLD.

## 6. After the call (2 minutes, non-negotiable)

Fill one row in [call-log.md](call-log.md) while it's fresh: score, best verbatim quote, objection, ask result, referrals. A call with no log row didn't happen.
